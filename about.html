What is Hidden Collections Africa?

The Council on Library and Information Resources (CLIR) proposes a new grant program to digitally preserve collections, promoting history and scholarship while decolonising knowledge spaces for Africa. This will be achieved by describing and digitising materials at organisations in Africa that are inaccessible or at risk of being lost forever. The initiative draws inspiration from CLIR's large-scale grant programs, including Digitizing Hidden Special Collections and Archives. Led by African-based professionals, the new proposed program, Hidden Collections Africa, would directly support institutions and staff across the African continent to meet urgent preservation needs, close gaps in the historical record, and make records more accessible to users.

Why is it needed?

The Universal Declaration of Human Rights asserts that access to information is a universal human right. It underpins social justice and facilitates informed decision making for planning, research, and development of humanity. In the 21st century, the availability of digital information has eased access to information for many. However, globally, Africa has the lowest number of accessible and available collections, both digital and print. Less than four percent of Africa’s collections are available digitally. The limited availability of digital resources about and by Africans hinders egalitarian access to information and knowledge advancement on the continent. This is evidenced by duplication in research, inaccurate research conclusions, misguided policies, and the exclusion of some communities in research agendas. It also reduces the authenticity of history and intergenerational knowledge sharing.

Meanwhile, collecting organizations in Africa face a number of urgent preservation challenges due to climate change and ongoing legacies of colonial exploitation. Rising sea levels, extreme heat, and aberrant weather patterns are on track to displace millions of people over the next several decades, increasing conflict and instability. Climate disasters, including fires, floods, desertification, and drought, threaten to obliterate these collections, some of which have already been lost. Inventories and digital copies of collections are important safeguards to ensure that some versions of the materials held in African repositories will remain available for future generations.

Africa’s collections are mainly available in oral and traditional hard copy formats. This has been due to a lack of resources, both human skills and infrastructure. Critical holdings in need of digitization include content related to political movements and leadership, climate and environment, women and gender, indigenous languages, and innumerable other topics. Equitable access to these African collections will help inform decisions in civil society, religious groups, and other related communities.

How would it work?

The program, as currently conceived, would focus on description, processing, and digitising hidden and at-risk materials held at collecting organizations in Africa. The program design will be refined in consultation with an advisory committee, made up of experts from different regions of Africa and beyond. The program draws from CLIR’s existing regranting programs and would be designed with local considerations in mind. As with CLIR’s other programs, this one would be organized around open competitions, with selections determined by an independent review panel. In the initial years, the program may limit eligibility by region, rotating geographic focus by program cycle. Hidden Collections Africa would group applicants into three pools by region: 1) Southern and Eastern Africa, 2) Central and West Africa, and 3) North Africa. The project will cover the four main languages: English, French, Arabic, and Portuguese collections, as well as indigenous African languages aligned with the regional focus of each cycle.

Open digital access to the collections and related resources would be encouraged, but not required, given the complexities around international intellectual property laws, particularly with recorded collections, and respecting diverse cultures. Flexibility around access requirements would also minimize the risks of the exploitation of sensitive materials and the records of marginalized communities. Preservation requirements of these unique collections is also critical. Therefore, factoring in and understanding preservation needs, for example, identifying paper types of lower quality, will allow prioritization of higher-risk collection materials.

To ensure sustainability of the project and other cascade positive effects, the project will support technical skills building and exchange. This will boost the number of African based professionals who are able to continue with digitisation processes and implementation in the future. In line with the global UN Sustainable Development Goals, this project will foster South to South and South to North partnerships and help extend the networks of professionals across the continent. The project also strengthens connections between African cultural organizations and the communities in the African diaspora.


Goals

  • To promote history and scholarship through Hidden Collections in Africa
  • To establish South to South and South to North networks, and strengthen connections between Africa and communities in the African diaspora
  • To provide intergenerational skills for digitisation in African professionals
  • To decolonize knowledge spaces


High Level Outcomes

  • Increased usage of the digital collections
  • Increased research outputs from the institutions
  • Improved understanding of indeginous knowledge systems
  • Higher pool of professionals with digitisation skills
  • Established sub-programmes and linkages
  • Facilitated digital repatriation
  • Improved inter-generational knowledge-sharing
  • A flexible, evolving, and reusable set of protocols, processes, and workflows that can be applied to and adopted by other parts of the world similarly threatened
  • A combination of training, workflows, efficient technical architecture and cross generational integration of resources into teaching and research as a new paradigm of long term sustainability of cultural heritage


Outputs

  • Digitally preserve records of Africa’s at-risk cultural heritage
  • Institutional repositories that are open andaccessible
  • Network of skilled professionals in digitisation
  • Infrastructure for sustained digitisation
  • Programmes developed around the collections
  • Collaborations and Partnerships for development in information and knowledge sharing


Activities

  • Establishing Review teams
  • Sub-granting for digitization projects
  • Liaising with grantees on project implementation
  • Coordination of the program
  • Monitoring and Evaluation of the program
  • Training of digitisation teams
  • Establishing collaborations and partnerships between African institutions and institutions outside of Africa
  • Marketing and Outreach of project (including process and outcomes)
  • Budgeting and financial management of grants


Assumptions

  • Buy in and support at institutional and association level
  • Political willingness and stability in the country and region
  • Skills and capacity will enable sustained digitisation
  • Interest and buy in from institutions and organisations outside Africa
