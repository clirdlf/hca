What is Hidden Collections Africa?

Hidden Collections Africa is a proposed new grant program by the Council on Library and Information Resources (CLIR) aimed at the digital preservation of collections, preservation of history and scholarhsip, and decolonisation of knowledge spaces for Africa. This program will focus on describing and digitizing materials at organizations in Africa that are at inaccessible, or at risk of being lost forever.

Led by African-based professionals, the program draws inspiration from CLIR's large-scale grant programs and will directly support institutions and staff across the continent to meet urgent preservation needs, close gabs in the historical record, and make records more accessible to users.

Why is it needed?

The Universal Declaration of Human Rights asserts that access to information is a universal human right. It underpins social justice and facilitates informed decision making for planning, research, and development of humanity. In the 21st century, the availability of digital information has eased access to information for many. However, globally, Africa has the lowest number of accessible and available collections, both digital and print. Less than four percent of Africa's collections are available digitally. The limited availability of digital resources about and by Africans hinders egalitarian access to information and knowledge advancement on the continent. This is evidenced by duplication in research, inaccurate research conclusions, misguided policies, and the exclusion of some communities in research agendas. It also reduces the authenticity of history and intergenerational knowledge sharing.

Meanwhile, collecting organizations in Africa face a number of urgent preservation challenges due to climate change and ongoing legacies of colonial exploitation. Rising sea levels, extreme heat, and aberrant weather patterns are on track to displace millions of people over the next several decades, increasing conflict and instability. Climate disasters, including fires, floods, desertification, and drought, threaten to obliterate these collections, some of which have already been lost. Inventories and digital copies of collections are important safeguards to ensure that some versions of the materials held in African repositories will remain available for future generations.

Africa's collections are mainly available in oral and traditional hard copy formats. This has been due to a lack of resources, both human skills and infrastructure. Critical holdings in need of digitization include content related to political movements and leadership, climate and environment, women and gender, indigenous languages, and innumerable other topics. Equitable access to these African collections will help inform decisions in civil society, religious groups, and other related communities.

Goals and Outcomes


Goals

  • Promote history and scholarship
  • Establish South to South and South to North networks, and strengthen connections between Africa and communities in the African diaspora
  • Provide intergenerational skills for digitisation in African professionals
  • Decolonize knowledge spaces


High Level Outcomes

  • Increase usage of the digital collections
  • Increase research outputs from the institutions
  • Improve understanding of indeginous knowledge systems
  • Grow pool of professionals with digitisation skills
  • Establish sub-programs and linkages
  • Facilitat digital repatriation
  • Improve inter-generational knowledge-sharing
  • Develop a flexible, evolving, and reusable set of protocols, processes, and workflows that can be applied to and adopted by other parts of the world similarly threatened
  • Provide a combination of training, workflows, efficient technical architecture and cross generational integration of resources into teaching and research as a new paradigm of long term sustainability of cultural heritage


Outputs

  • Digitally preserve records of Africa's at-risk cultural heritage
  • Encourage institutional repositories that are open andaccessible
  • Grow network of skilled professionals in digitisation
  • Build infrastructure for sustained digitisation
  • Develop programs around the digitized collections
  • Build collaborations and Partnerships for development in information and knowledge sharing
 
 
 
 
 
Phase 1

Planning and Stakeholder Recruitment

Program partners work together to raise public awareness about the need for Hidden Collections Africa and to secure funding for its pilot implementation.

Phase 2

Pilot open application period

Partners adapt and implement guidelines for applicants while issuing a limited call for proposals.

 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
Phase 3

Pilot grantee implementation

The program’s first grant recipients describe and digitize hidden collections and make them available online.

Phase 4

Evaluation

Partners work with staff and grant recipients to document the program’s impact, improve and expand support for participants, and raise funds for subsequent calls for proposals.
